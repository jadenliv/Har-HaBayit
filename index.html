Section I

The Kedusha Demands Our Presence

Har HaBayit is not merely a historical site. It is the spiritual epicenter of creation, the place where Heaven and Earth meet, the location the Creator chose as His dwelling among men. The kedusha of this place is so immense and so permanent that it calls out to us — it demands that we orient ourselves toward it, seek it out, and go there. The very holiness of the Makom is the reason to ascend, not the reason to stay away.

A. Even HaShtiyah — The Foundation Stone

Talmud Bavli
Yoma 54b
תניא, רבי אליעזר אומר: עולם מאמצעיתו נברא... אבן אחת היתה שם, וממנה הושתת העולם... ולמה נקרא שמה אבן שתייה? שממנה הושתת העולם.
It was taught, Rabbi Eliezer says: The world was created from its center... There was a stone there, and from it the world was founded... And why is it called the Foundation Stone? Because from it the world was founded.

The Even HaShtiyah is the point from which all creation emanated. This is not metaphor — Chazal understood it as a real place with real spiritual significance. The place where creation began is the place we are drawn to return.

Yoma 54b

B. The Eternal Altar — From Adam to the Mikdash

Rambam
Hilchot Beit HaBechira 2:1–2
מקום המזבח מכוון ביותר, ואינו משתנה לעולם... ומקובל ביד כל ישראל שהמקום שבנה דוד ושלמה בגורן ארונה הוא המקום שעקד בו אברהם ובנה בו המזבח ועקד בו יצחק, והוא המקום שבנה בו נח כשיצא מן התיבה, והוא המקום שהקריב בו קין והבל, ושם הקריב אדם הראשון קרבן כשנברא, ומשם נברא.
The location of the Altar is extremely precise and it never changes... It is a tradition held by all Israel that the place where David and Shlomo built the Altar on the threshing floor of Aravna is the same place where Avraham built the Altar and bound Yitzchak, and the same place where Noach built after leaving the Ark, and the same where Kayin and Hevel offered, and where Adam HaRishon offered a korban when he was created — and from there he was created.

Every generation from Adam onward was drawn to this spot. The Rambam is teaching us that the pull toward this Makom is woven into creation itself. The line of avodah is unbroken: Adam, Noach, Avraham, David, Shlomo — and us.

Rambam Beit HaBechira 2:1

C. The Kedusha is Eternal — The Shechina Never Departed

Rambam
Hilchot Beit HaBechira 6:15–16
קדושת המקדש וקדושת ירושלים קדושתן לעולם... ולמה אני אומר שקדושת המקדש וירושלים קדשה לעולם? מפני שקדושת המקדש וירושלים מפני השכינה, ושכינה אינה בטלה.
The sanctity of the Mikdash and of Yerushalayim endures forever... And why do I say that the kedusha of the Mikdash and Yerushalayim is eternal? Because the kedusha of the Mikdash and Yerushalayim derives from the Shechina, and the Shechina is never nullified.

The Shechina is still there. The walls fell, the stones were scattered, the enemy occupies the surface — but the Shechina remains. If the Shechina never left, how can we?

Rambam Beit HaBechira 6:15

D. "Seek His Dwelling and Go There"

Torah
Devarim 12:5
כי אם אל המקום אשר יבחר ה׳ אלהיכם מכל שבטיכם לשום את שמו שם, לשכנו תדרשו ובאת שמה.
Rather, to the place that Hashem your God will choose from all your tribes to place His Name there — seek out His dwelling-place and go there.

This is a positive command. Lishkhno tidreshu — SEEK it. U'vata shama — and GO there. The Torah does not say "wait." It does not say "avoid." It says seek and go.

Devarim 12:5
Rambam
Sefer HaMitzvot, Aseh 20; Hilchot Beit HaBechira 1:1
מצות עשה לעשות בית לה׳
It is a positive commandment to build a House for Hashem.

The Rambam counts the building of the Mikdash as a mitzvat aseh. The drive to build, to seek, to be present at the Makom — this is commanded, not optional.

Rambam Sefer HaMitzvot Aseh 20

E. The Mapping of Heaven and Earth

Talmud Bavli
Zevachim 116b

The Gemara establishes that the tripartite division of machanot (Yisrael, Leviya, Shechina) that operated in the Midbar maps directly onto the permanent geography of Yerushalayim and the Mikdash.

What applied in the Midbar applies forever in Yerushalayim. The structure of kedusha is not historical — it is eternal. The geography of Har HaBayit is the architecture of the relationship between Hashem and His people.

Zevachim 116b
Section II

The Three Machanot and Their Halachic Mapping

The Torah divides the encampment into three concentric zones of ascending kedusha. Each zone has distinct restrictions on who may enter while in a state of tumah. Understanding this structure is the key to the entire she'elah.

Machane In the Midbar In Yerushalayim Who is Excluded
Machane Yisrael Israelite camp Walled city of Yerushalayim Metzora only
Machane Leviya Levite camp Har HaBayit (Temple Mount) Metzora, Zav/Zavah
Machane Shechina Mishkan courtyard The Azara (Temple courtyard) All above + Tamei Met

A. The Torah Source

Torah
Bamidbar 5:1–4
צו את בני ישראל וישלחו מן המחנה כל צרוע וכל זב וכל טמא לנפש.
Command the Children of Israel that they send out from the camp every metzora, every zav, and everyone who is tamei from a dead body.

The Torah establishes three distinct categories of tumah with three distinct levels of exclusion. Chazal derive the three machanot from these distinctions.

B. The Gemara

Talmud Bavli
Pesachim 67a
טמא מת משתלח חוץ לשתי מחנות... זב וזבה משתלחין חוץ לשלש מחנות... מצורע משתלח חוץ לכל שלש מחנות.
A tamei met is sent out of only two machanot [permitted in Machane Yisrael]... A zav and zavah are sent out of three machanot [permitted only outside all camps]... A metzora is sent out of all three machanot.

The Gemara establishes definitively: a tamei met is excluded ONLY from Machane Shechina (the Azara). He remains permitted in Machane Leviya (Har HaBayit) and Machane Yisrael (Yerushalayim).

Pesachim 67a

C. The Rambam's Codification

Rambam
Hilchot Biat HaMikdash 3:3–5
טמא מת מותר ליכנס להר הבית... ואם נכנס לעזרה חייב כרת.
A tamei met is permitted to enter Har HaBayit [Machane Leviya]... but if he enters the Azara [Machane Shechina] he is liable for karet.

This is the linchpin. The Rambam rules clearly: Har HaBayit = Machane Leviya. A tamei met is PERMITTED there. The karet prohibition applies ONLY to the Azara. Since every Jew alive today is presumed tamei met (we lack a parah adumah), the crucial question is simply: can we identify where the Azara is so as to avoid it?

Rambam Biat HaMikdash 3:3
⚠ Critical Practical Point

Although a tamei met may enter Machane Leviya, a ba'al keri (one who has had a seminal emission) is excluded even from Machane Leviya (Rambam Biat HaMikdash 3:3). Therefore, immersion in a mikveh before ascending is an absolute requirement according to all mattir poskim. Similarly, a niddah or zavah is excluded from Machane Leviya. This is not a chumra — it is baseline halacha.

Section III

The Boundaries Are Knowable

The central objection of those who prohibit ascending is that we cannot determine the boundaries of the Azara with certainty, and since the penalty is karet, we must err on the side of caution. The mattir position must demonstrate — and does demonstrate — that the boundaries are knowable.

A. Mishnah Middot — The Measurements

Mishnah
Middot 2:1
הר הבית היה חמש מאות אמה על חמש מאות אמה. רוב מדתו מן הדרום, מן המזרח, מן הצפון, מן המערב.
Har HaBayit was 500 amot by 500 amot. The largest space was to the south, then to the east, then to the north, then to the west.

The Mishnah gives us precise measurements. Combined with the statement that the greatest open space was to the south, this allows us to calculate the position of the Azara within the larger Har HaBayit compound. The southern portion of Har HaBayit is the farthest from the Azara by all calculations.

Middot 2:1

B. The Radvaz — Location of Even HaShtiyah

Rishon / Acharon
Radvaz (Rabbi David ibn Zimra), Responsa 2:691

The Radvaz discusses the location of the Even HaShtiyah and identifies it with the rock under the Dome of the Rock, based on both Jewish tradition and the consistent testimony of Arab tradition.

A major Rishon-era posek affirms that the core geography was identifiable in his time. The identification of the rock under the Dome has remained consistent from the Radvaz through the present day. This anchors all subsequent calculations.

C. Kaftor VaFerach (R' Ishtori HaParchi, 14th c.)

Rishon
Kaftor VaFerach, Chapter 6

R' Ishtori HaParchi visited Eretz Yisrael and conducted detailed study of the physical dimensions and geography of Har HaBayit, working from Mishnah Middot and direct observation. He provides measurements and identifications of the site's features.

One of the earliest post-Rambam authorities to physically engage with the site. His work is foundational for mapping halachic boundaries onto physical geography.

D. Rav Shlomo Goren — Modern Mapping

Contemporary
Rav Shlomo Goren, Sefer Har HaBayit (1992)

After the liberation of the Old City in 1967, Rav Goren conducted extensive research combining Middot, archaeological evidence, and topographic analysis. He concluded that specific pathways on Har HaBayit are certainly outside the Azara and are therefore permissible for a tamei met who has immersed.

Rav Goren's work is the most comprehensive modern study mapping halachic boundaries onto the physical site. His methodology is rigorous and draws on all available evidence.

The commonly cited figure that the Azara comprises approximately 13% of the total Har HaBayit surface area is a modern calculation derived from cross-referencing Middot with archaeological surveys. It is not from Chazal, but it is based on their measurements.

Section IV

Historical Precedent for Ascending

A. The Rambam Himself Went Up

Rambam
Iggeret (Letter, dated 1165 CE)
נכנסנו לבית הגדול והקדוש והתפללנו שם... ואותו יום שנכנסנו לבית הגדול, עשינוהו יום טוב.
We entered the great and holy House and prayed there... And that day on which we entered the great House, we made a personal yom tov.

The Rambam — the very posek who codified the laws of tumah, the machanot, and the penalties for unauthorized entry — ascended Har HaBayit himself. He clearly held that entry was permissible with knowledge of the boundaries. His actions are the most powerful precedent.

This letter is published in Iggrot HaRambam (ed. Sheilat), Vol. 1.

B. The Meiri

Rishon
Meiri, Beit HaBechira on Shevuot 16a

The Meiri discusses the status of the site and engages with the question of ongoing kedusha as a live halachic matter. His treatment implies familiarity with entry to certain areas of the Har in the Rishonim period.

Further evidence that post-Churban entry was not universally prohibited. The blanket prohibition often assumed to be ancient is in fact a relatively late development.

C. Multiple Medieval Accounts

Multiple accounts from the medieval period describe Jewish entry onto Har HaBayit, including accounts from the Crusader era and Mamluk period. Jewish presence on the Har was documented by travelers and halachic authorities over centuries. The blanket prohibition became dominant primarily after the Chief Rabbinate's ruling following 1967.

Section V

Mora Mikdash — Awe That Presupposes Entry

Torah
Vayikra 19:30
את שבתתי תשמרו ומקדשי תיראו אני ה׳.
You shall guard My Shabbatot and revere My Mikdash — I am Hashem.

The mitzvah of mora mikdash — awe and reverence for the Mikdash — is a positive commandment.

Vayikra 19:30
Rambam
Hilchot Beit HaBechira 7:1–7
מצות עשה לירא מן המקדש... לא יכנס להר הבית במקלו, ולא במנעלו, ולא באבק שעל רגליו, ולא בפונדקו, ולא יעשנו קפנדריא.
It is a positive commandment to revere the Mikdash... One may not enter Har HaBayit with his walking stick, or with his shoes, or with dust on his feet, or with his money-belt, or make it a shortcut.
Rambam Beit HaBechira 7:1
Key Insight

The mattir poskim make a critical observation: Mora mikdash is a mitzvah that presupposes entry. The Rambam is telling you HOW to enter Har HaBayit — without shoes, without a stick, without a money-belt, without making it a shortcut. These are instructions for someone who IS entering. You cannot fulfill mora mikdash from outside. If the Torah forbade all entry, these halachot would be meaningless. The Torah is commanding reverent entry, not avoidance.

Talmud Bavli
Brachot 54a

One may not enter Har HaBayit with his shoes on. The Gemara discusses the conduct required on the Har — additional Talmudic source for the practical halachot of how one conducts oneself ON Har HaBayit, presupposing entry.

Rambam
Hilchot Beit HaBechira 7:7
אף על פי שהמקדש חרב, מצוה עלינו לנהוג בו במורא ויראה.
Even though the Mikdash is destroyed, we are commanded to conduct ourselves there with awe and reverence.

The Rambam explicitly states: EVEN IN DESTRUCTION, the mitzvah of mora mikdash applies. The mitzvah is current, active, and obligatory. A mitzvah that requires conduct at the site necessarily requires presence at the site.

Section VI

Additional Mitzvot Connected to the Site

A. Yishuv Eretz Yisrael and Sovereignty

Ramban
Hasagot to Sefer HaMitzvot, Aseh 4
שנצטוינו לרשת את הארץ... שלא נעזבה ביד אומה אחרת מן האומות.
We were commanded to inherit the Land... and not to leave it in the hands of any other nation.

The Ramban counts yishuv Eretz Yisrael as a mitzvat aseh min HaTorah, including the dimension of sovereignty — not leaving the Land in the hands of another nation. Voluntary Jewish absence from the holiest site while non-Jews access it freely raises serious concerns.

B. Lo Techanem

Rambam
Hilchot Avodah Zarah 10:3–4
לא תחנם... לא תיתן להם חנייה בקרקע.
Lo techanem — do not give them a foothold in the Land.

Some poskim apply this principle to Har HaBayit: ceding effective control of the site to non-Jewish authority may implicate this prohibition.

C. Korban Pesach and Karet

Torah
Bamidbar 9:13
והאיש אשר הוא טהור ודרך לא היה וחדל לעשות הפסח ונכרתה הנפש ההוא מעמיה.
And the person who is tahor and was not on a journey, and yet fails to bring the Pesach offering — that soul shall be cut off from its people.

The Torah prescribes karet for failing to bring the Korban Pesach when able. Whether this obligation applies today is disputed, but the she'elah itself underscores the gravity of what is at stake: blanket avoidance of the site may not only forfeit positive mitzvot but potentially incur karet for their neglect.

Bamidbar 9:13
Rambam
Hilchot Beit HaBechira 6:15 + Hilchot Korban Pesach 1:1

If the kedusha of the Makom endures (as the Rambam rules), and if the requirement to bring korbanot is tied to the Makom rather than the structure, the theoretical obligation to bring the Korban Pesach may persist today. This position has been discussed by the Chazon Ish and others, though it remains a matter of active machloket.

This is not settled halacha. But the mattir poskim raise it to illustrate the depth of what is potentially at stake: we may not only be forgoing optional merit but actively failing a Torah obligation.

Section VII

The Opposing View — Those Who Prohibit

Intellectual honesty requires engaging seriously with the opposing position. The prohibition of ascending is held by significant poskim. Their reasoning must be understood and addressed.

A. Rav Ovadia Yosef

Contemporary
Yabia Omer, Vol. 5, Yoreh De'ah 26

Rav Ovadia rules that one should NOT ascend Har HaBayit. His primary concerns: (1) The exact boundaries of the Azara cannot be determined with sufficient certainty; (2) Since the punishment for entering the Azara while tamei met is karet, we must err on the side of caution (safek karet lechumra); (3) Practical difficulty of ensuring proper preparation for all who go up; (4) Mora mikdash counsels against casual entry.

Rav Ovadia's approach is characteristically conservative on matters of potential karet. His position must be taken seriously.

B. The Chief Rabbinate Ban (1967)

Immediately after the Six-Day War, the Chief Rabbinate issued a statement prohibiting Jews from ascending. This was signed by both Chief Rabbis and reaffirmed multiple times. The reasoning overlapped with Rav Ovadia's concerns: uncertainty about boundaries, severity of karet, and concerns about public compliance.

C. The Chareidi Consensus

The broad Chareidi consensus prohibits ascending. Poskim including Rav Elyashiv, students of the Chazon Ish, and the Eidah Chareidit maintain this position. The Chareidi argument often adds a theological dimension: that asserting sovereignty before Mashiach is inappropriate. This is a hashkafic argument, not a strictly halachic one.

D. Responses to the Opposing View

On boundary uncertainty:

The Radvaz, Kaftor VaFerach, and Rav Goren have demonstrated that the boundaries are knowable. Modern archaeology has only increased certainty. The Rambam himself ascended, demonstrating confidence in boundary identification. The southern portion of Har HaBayit is certainly outside the Azara by ALL opinions and calculations.

On safek karet:

This principle applies where there is genuine doubt. Where an area has been positively identified as outside the Azara, there is no safek. Walking in the southern section of Har HaBayit is not a safek karet any more than walking anywhere else in Yerushalayim.

On practical compliance:

The mattir poskim require specific preparation: tevila, removal of leather shoes, restricted routes, proper kavanah. The argument that people will not comply is a reason to educate, not a reason to prohibit.

On the theological argument:

The claim that we must wait for Mashiach before ascending has no formal halachic basis. The Rambam ascended. The Ramban counted sovereignty as a mitzvah. The return to Eretz Yisrael and Yerushalayim is itself part of the geulah process, not something that only begins after it is complete.

Section VIII

Meta-Halachic Arguments

A. The Taz — A Gezeira Cannot Permanently Uproot a Mitzvah

Acharon
Taz, Orach Chaim 588

The Taz discusses the principle that a rabbinic gezeira cannot permanently abolish a Torah mitzvah. His context is tekiat shofar when Rosh HaShanah falls on Shabbat, where Chazal suspended shofar blowing to prevent carrying.

The mattir poskim apply this principle: if there are Torah mitzvot connected to Har HaBayit (mora mikdash, lishkhno tidreshu, potentially korbanot), a blanket rabbinic prohibition that bars Jews from the site indefinitely may exceed the legitimate scope of a gezeira. A gezeira is meant to be a fence around a mitzvah, not a wall that replaces it.

Caveat: The Taz himself does not apply this to Har HaBayit. The application is by analogy. But the principle is well-established, and several contemporary poskim have invoked it.

B. Authority: Gedolim vs. Sanhedrin

Rambam
Hilchot Mamrim 1:1–2
בית דין הגדול שבירושלים הם עיקר תורה שבעל פה, והם עמודי ההוראה ומהן הדת והמשפט יוצאים לכל ישראל.
The Great Beit Din in Yerushalayim is the foundation of the Oral Torah. They are the pillars of instruction, and from them law and judgment go forth to all Israel.

Binding national halachic legislation (gezeirot and takkanot obligating all Israel) can only be enacted by a Sanhedrin or a Beit Din accepted by all Israel. Individual poskim — however great — do not have the formal authority to enact permanent national prohibitions. The Chief Rabbinate ban and the consensus of individual Gedolim, while deserving great respect, do not carry the formal weight of a Sanhedrin-level decree.

Rambam Hilchot Mamrim 1:1

C. The Lifnei Iver Consideration

A practical meta-argument: Jews are ascending Har HaBayit in growing numbers regardless of rabbinic rulings. If the only people who comply with the prohibition are the halachically-committed — exactly those who WOULD prepare properly with tevila and proper conduct — while those who disregard rabbinic authority go up unprepared, the prohibition becomes counterproductive. Providing proper halachic guidance may be the more responsible approach. The careful Jews stay away; the careless ones go up without preparation. Who benefits from this arrangement?

Section IX

Contemporary Poskim Who Permit

The following poskim have ruled that ascending Har HaBayit is permitted with proper preparation. This is not a fringe position — it is a legitimate halachic view held by serious talmidei chachamim.

  • Rav Shlomo Goren (1917–2005)
    Former Ashkenazi Chief Rabbi of Israel and former IDF Chief Rabbi. Conducted the most extensive modern study of the site. Published Sefer Har HaBayit with detailed halachic-geographic analysis.
  • Rav Dov Lior
    Chief Rabbi of Kiryat Arba–Hevron. Senior dati leumi posek who has repeatedly ruled that ascending is permissible with proper preparation.
  • Rav Yisrael Ariel
    Head of Machon HaMikdash (Temple Institute). Has written extensively on permissibility and published detailed maps of permissible routes.
  • Rav She'ar Yashuv Cohen (1927–2016)
    Former Chief Rabbi of Haifa. Supported permissibility with proper preparation.
  • Rav Nachum Rabinovitch (1928–2020)
    Rosh Yeshivat Birkat Moshe (Ma'ale Adumim). Major halachic authority who ruled in favor with conditions.
  • Rav Chaim Druckman (1932–2022)
    Senior dati leumi figure who supported ascending Har HaBayit.
The Rambam as Ultimate Precedent

Rabbeinu Moshe ben Maimon ascended Har HaBayit in 1165 and made it a yom tov. He is the authority who codified the laws of tumah, the machanot, and the penalties for unauthorized entry. His practice speaks louder than any theoretical prohibition.

Section X

Practical Requirements for Ascending

According to the mattir poskim, the following are REQUIRED — not optional — conditions for ascending Har HaBayit.

  • Tevila in a Mikveh
    Immersion in a kosher mikveh on the day of ascent. This addresses tum'at ba'al keri and other tum'ot that bar entry even to Machane Leviya. Non-negotiable. (Rambam, Biat HaMikdash 3:3)
  • Removal of Leather Shoes
    One may not enter Har HaBayit wearing leather shoes. (Rambam, Beit HaBechira 7:2; Brachot 54a)
  • No Unnecessary Items
    One should not enter with a wallet, walking stick, backpack, or similar items. Entry should be purposeful. (Rambam, Beit HaBechira 7:2–3)
  • Restricted Routes Only
    One must follow routes identified as certainly outside the Azara. The standard route follows the southern and eastern edges. One must NOT walk into the central/northern area near the Dome of the Rock. (Rav Goren, Sefer Har HaBayit; Machon HaMikdash maps)
  • Proper Kavanah and Mora Mikdash
    Entry should be with yir'at shamayim, tefillah, and consciousness of the kedusha. Mora mikdash is a mitzvah to be fulfilled at the site. (Rambam, Beit HaBechira 7:1, 7:7)
  • Women: Niddah Status
    A woman in a state of niddah is excluded from Machane Leviya (categorized with zavah). A woman who has counted properly, immersed, and is tehorah may ascend. This is a significant practical limitation.
  • Not Using the Har as a Shortcut
    One may not enter the Har merely to cut through to another location. Entry must be for the sake of the Makom itself. (Rambam, Beit HaBechira 7:2)
Section XI

Summary of the Argument

  1. The kedusha of the Makom HaMikdash is eternal. The Shechina never departed. The place that drew Adam, Noach, Avraham, and David draws us still.

    Rambam, Beit HaBechira 6:15–16; Yoma 54b

  2. The Torah commands us to seek out the place of the Shechina and go there.

    Devarim 12:5; Rambam, Sefer HaMitzvot Aseh 20

  3. The tripartite machane structure maps onto Yerushalayim: Yerushalayim = Machane Yisrael, Har HaBayit = Machane Leviya, the Azara = Machane Shechina.

    Pesachim 67a; Zevachim 116b

  4. A tamei met — which includes every Jew alive today — is prohibited from entering the Azara but IS PERMITTED to enter Har HaBayit (Machane Leviya).

    Rambam, Biat HaMikdash 3:4–5

  5. Other forms of tumah (ba'al keri, niddah, zav) DO restrict entry to Machane Leviya, and therefore immersion in a mikveh is required.

    Rambam, Biat HaMikdash 3:3

  6. The boundaries of the Azara are knowable through Mishnah Middot, the Radvaz, Kaftor VaFerach, archaeological evidence, and Rav Goren's comprehensive research.

  7. The Rambam himself ascended Har HaBayit in 1165 CE and made it a personal yom tov. Additional Rishonim-era evidence supports the practice.

    Iggrot HaRambam

  8. Mora Mikdash is a mitzvah that presupposes entry. The Torah commands conduct ON the Har. Even in destruction, this mitzvah applies.

    Rambam, Beit HaBechira 7:1, 7:7

  9. Multiple Torah mitzvot are connected to the site (mora mikdash, lishkhno tidreshu, yishuv ha'aretz), and a blanket prohibition preventing their fulfillment indefinitely is difficult to sustain.

    Taz, OC 588

  10. The authority to enact binding national prohibitions belongs to the Sanhedrin, not individual poskim.

    Rambam, Hilchot Mamrim 1–2

  11. The opposing view — led by Rav Ovadia Yosef, the Chief Rabbinate, and the Chareidi consensus — is based on genuine concerns about boundary uncertainty and karet. These concerns are addressed by the mattir poskim but deserve serious engagement.

  12. Ascending Har HaBayit with proper preparation is a legitimate halachic position held by major poskim, grounded in Rishonim and Gemara, and supported by the Rambam's own practice.
